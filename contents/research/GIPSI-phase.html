Financial Crises as Phase Transitions

Our model has a stable phase and an unstable phase where we either get a "bubble" or a financial crisis.

The response of price and holdings to positive and negative shocks in the red and blue phases. In the red phase (left two plots) neither negative (first plot from left) nor positive (second plot) shocks result in much change in the system. In the blue, unstable phase, however (right two plots) a negative shock (third plot) results in a crash and a positive shock (fourth plot) results in exponential growth (bubble).

 

Left: The phase diagram of the GIPSI model. The two behavioral parameters "inverse proce elasticity," $\alpha$, and the "income elasticity of demand," $\beta$ are the phase variables. The red phase is the stable phase and the blue phase is unstable. Right: The relaxation time explodes near the phase transition, alluding to a second order type phase transition.

 

Using a mean-field approach, we show that our systemic risk model used on the Eurozone crisis exhibits a stable and an unstable phase. The unstable phase can result in either an exponentially growing bubble (if the system receives any positive shock) or crash exponentially, resulting in a financial crisis (if the shock is negative).

We derive the phase transition between these two phases analytically.
